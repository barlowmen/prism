# Section: Career narrative & positioning thesis

## Canonical heading

`## Career narrative & positioning thesis`

## What this section captures

The user's **one-line story** (the elevator answer to "what do you do") and their **differentiating thesis** (the interesting problem they care about that separates them from peers). Resume summaries and cover letters pull from this directly.

## Questions to cover

1. **One-line story.** Ask: "If a senior leader at one of your target companies asked 'what do you do?', what's the answer in one sentence?" If the answer is generic ("I'm an AI engineer"), push for specifics until it's concrete and differentiated.
2. **The interesting problem.** What's a hard problem in your space that you find genuinely interesting and have credibility to talk about? Probe: "Why is this *the* problem you care about, not just an interesting one?" Look for a thesis the user can defend in an interview, not buzzwords.
3. **Differentiation.** Who are the *other people* applying to your target roles, and what's the dimension on which you're different? (Not better — different.) E.g., "Most platform leaders haven't shipped production AI. Most AI researchers haven't run a 50-person org."
4. **How to deploy per archetype.** For each priority archetype from the previous section, where does the thesis go on the resume? Top of summary? Education section? Cover letter hook? This becomes the playbook for the drafting agent.

## Output structure (draft format)

```
## Career narrative & positioning thesis

### The one-line story
> *"<the user's exact words>"*

### The thesis (use this freely; it's <name>'s, not made up)
<2–4 sentence paragraph in the user's voice, capturing the interesting problem and why they're credible on it.>

This narrative differentiates <name> from:
- *<Adjacent peer group 1>* (who care about <X>, not <Y>)
- *<Adjacent peer group 2>* (who care about <X>, not <Y>)
- *<Adjacent peer group 3>* (who care about <X>, not <Y>)

### How to deploy the thesis on resumes
- **<Archetype A>:** <where it goes, how it's framed>
- **<Archetype B>:** <…>
- **<Archetype C>:** <…>
```

## Hard rule for this section

The thesis must be in the **user's own words**. Don't paraphrase. If they give you "AI is changing how organizations work," push back — that's a platitude. Keep asking until they say something specific enough that a peer in their field would recognize it as *their* angle.
